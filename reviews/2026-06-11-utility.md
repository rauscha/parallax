# Parallax — Utility Review

**Date:** 2026-06-11 · **Lens:** real-world usefulness, helpfulness, multipurpose value · **Branch:** `claude/codebase-shipping-review-q5whpr`

Method: read the data files, the explain/match/share/MIDI code paths, and the firmware they claim to describe (`dsp/vendor/braids/settings.h` / `settings.cc`), then judged each user persona against what the code actually does today.

---

## 1. Verdict

**Utility grade: B+** (A− within its niche, B− as a general music tool).

**This is genuinely useful for one specific person who really exists: someone who wants to understand, audition, or learn the Mutable Instruments macro-oscillators — Braids' 47 models and Plaits' 24 engines — without owning the hardware, owning a DAW, or installing anything.** No other tool combines the *authentic firmware DSP* with a *per-model manual that knows which model you're on* and a one-tap "Show me" demonstration of every knob. That combination is the utility core, and it's executed well. The walls (no audio out, mono, 4 bars) keep it from being a production tool — but it never claimed to be one, and most of those walls are explicitly deferred by design.

---

## 2. Persona-by-persona analysis

### 2a. Eurorack owners learning their module — **the strongest case (A)**

The hardware Braids displays a 4-character code on a tiny screen ("`\x88\x8A\x8C\x8D`" rendered as glyphs — see `dsp/vendor/braids/settings.cc`, `algo_values[]`) and the TIMBRE/COLOR knobs mean something completely different in every one of 47 models. The physical workflow is "turn knob, guess, consult PDF." Parallax replaces that with:

- A per-model card stating exactly what TIMBRE and COLOR do *right now* (`src/data/braids-models.ts:50-426`), with live percentage readouts mirroring the patch (`src/ui/ExplainPanel.svelte:50,107`).
- A **"Show me" button per knob** that sweeps the parameter across its full range over 2.6 s while holding a demo note (`src/state/show-me.ts:36-50`) — the single highest-educational-value interaction in the app, because it answers "what does this knob *sound like*" rather than "what does the manual say."
- Knob ↔ card highlighting so touching a knob lights its explanation (`src/ui/ExplainPanel.svelte:56-66`).
- The model index in the data file is the firmware's actual enum value (`braids-models.ts:27`, verified against `settings.h:37-95` — ordering matches exactly, including the KICK/CYMB/SNAR drum order), so what you learn here transfers 1:1 to the hardware's model list.

**What's missing for this persona:** nothing structural. The knob percentages even double as a "recipe card" — a hardware owner can read 68%/33% off the Explain panel and set their module's knobs to match. (See §5: this is under-leveraged.)

**Audience size:** small in absolute terms (Eurorack is a niche), but Braids/Plaits are among the most-cloned modules in the ecosystem (After Later, Codesmith, countless DIY builds), and *every* owner faces the "what does TIMBRE do in this model" problem. High intent, durable need — this is a reference tool, not a toy, for them.

### 2b. Synth-curious people auditioning before buying — **strong (A−)**

Free, zero-install, runs on a phone, and the DSP is the actual MIT-licensed firmware compiled to WASM — not an approximation. Someone deciding between a Plaits clone and something else can audition all 24 engines (`src/data/plaits-models.ts`), hear the 6-op FM banks, the modal resonator, the chord engine, with honest macro behavior. The family grouping (`braids-models.ts:434-445`) makes a 47-model list browsable. Gap: no side-by-side A/B of two models, and no way to know how the module behaves under CV modulation (the thing Eurorack buyers actually buy it for) — "Show me" sweeps are a partial stand-in.

### 2c. Music educators / synthesis learners — **good with caveats (B)**

The synthesis-education content is real (assessed in §4). The melody side: staff notation with key signatures and correct flat/sharp spelling (`src/sequencer/scales.ts:63-88` — `snapAtPosition` keeps a clicked B-line as B♭ in F major rather than jumping to C), snap-to-scale on by default, a grid alternative for non-readers, and a Surprise-me that always generates *in-key* melodies (`src/state/surprise.ts:83-86`). That's a legitimate intervals-and-scales teaching surface.

Caveats: 4 bars, monophonic, one voice — you can teach "what is a scale" and "what is a wavefolder," not harmony or arrangement. There is no guided-lesson layer; the app teaches by exploration only. For a classroom, the shareable URL (teacher posts a link, every student opens the identical patch+melody — `src/state/share-url.ts`) is quietly the best feature, and it works offline as a PWA on school hardware.

### 2d. Sound designers sketching — **functional until the wall (B−)**

The "Match a sound" panel is more substantive than its name suggests: drop in a track, drag-select a region, loop it, and the app runs real DSP analysis — autocorrelation pitch detection, spectral-centroid brightness, RMS envelope classification (`src/audio/sample-analysis.ts:72-205`) — then ranks all ~80 models across three engines with an explainable scoring function (`src/audio/suggest.ts:101-144`) and applies the winner with macro nudges (`src/ui/MatchPanel.svelte:42-60`). The dual TARGET/YOUR-PATCH spectrum view for refining by eye is a genuinely good workflow. Honest framing in the code itself: "a heuristic starting point, NOT an optimizer" (`suggest.ts:14-15`).

But this persona hits the hardest wall in the app: **after dialing in the sound, it cannot leave as audio** (§3). A sound designer's deliverable is a WAV. Today their options are a share URL (useless to a DAW), a melody-only .mid (loses the sound entirely), or system screen-recording. This is the persona for whom the deferred audio-export decision hurts most.

### 2e. Musicians on the couch with a MIDI controller — **surprisingly solid (B+)**

Web MIDI input is properly done, not a checkbox: last-note priority with a held-note stack so releasing the top note falls back to the note under it instead of silence, sustain-pedal (CC64) support, panic on blur/visibility-change, hot-plug device handling (`src/state/midi-input.ts:34-158`). Velocity is passed through to the engine (`midi-input.ts:56`). With Plaits' 6-op FM banks or the modal resonator under your fingers, this is a real (mono) instrument.

Gaps: no iOS/Safari (Web MIDI unsupported — gracefully handled, `midi-input.ts:21-22`); pitch bend and CCs other than sustain are explicitly dropped (`midi-input.ts:124`), so the macro knobs — the entire point of these synths — can't be ridden from hardware (§5, §6); and what you play can't be captured (no recording, and played notes don't land in the sequencer).

### 2f. People who want a toy — **excellent (A)**

"Surprise me" rolls a coherent instrument — engine (which flips the whole visual theme), model, musically-clamped params that avoid silent/blasting/harsh extremes (`src/state/surprise.ts:26-49`), and a fresh in-key melody. Combined with the postcard, the themes, the scope, and tap-to-place melodies on a phone, the delight loop is short and real. This is also the funnel persona: a toy user who hits the Explain panel becomes persona 2c.

## 3. Workflow walls, ranked by pain

1. **Audio cannot get out.** *(Deferred by design — but it is THE wall, so saying so explicitly.)* Every persona except the pure Eurorack-reference user eventually wants the sound itself: the sound designer needs a WAV, the couch musician wants to keep a take, the toy user wants to post a video with sound. The postcard is a picture of a sound — the most shareable artifact the app makes is the one thing that's silent. This is the single deferred item whose absence caps the utility grade. (An honest note: a `MediaRecorder` tap on the existing output node is a small lift compared to what's already built, if the decision is ever revisited.)

2. **The postcard doesn't carry the patch.** `renderPostcard` footers the static domain string, not the share URL (`src/ui/postcard.ts:244`), and `PostcardModal` offers only PNG download/copy (`src/ui/PostcardModal.svelte:93-118`). Someone who sees a postcard cannot *load* that sound. The share-URL machinery already exists one file away (`share-url.ts:38-42`); the artifact and the link just never meet. Cheap fix, large utility loss as shipped.

3. **MIDI export is melody-only and flat.** The .mid is clean — tick-accurate 16ths, tempo header, correct round-trip (`src/sequencer/midi/convert.ts:30-48`) — but every note exports at hardcoded velocity 0.8 (`convert.ts:21,44`) and the file carries nothing about the *sound*: no track-name/text meta describing engine/model/knob values. A DAW user importing `parallax.mid` gets the notes and loses the patch; they must re-open the share URL to even read the settings. Import, by contrast, is honestly lossy and *says so* — quantize/mono/4-bar reduction with a dropped-note count surfaced in the UI (`convert.ts:62-99`, `src/ui/MidiMenu.svelte:55`). Good.

4. **Presets are trapped in one browser.** The library is IndexedDB-only (`src/state/persistence.ts:17`) — clear site data, or switch machines, and they're gone. The wire format is already a compact string shared with the URL encoder (`persistence.ts:45`); there's no export-all/import-all to a file. One bad browser cleanup wipes a user's collected work.

5. **Hardware MIDI is notes-only.** No CC→macro mapping (`midi-input.ts:124` drops everything but CC64), so a controller with knobs — i.e., most of them — can't perform TIMBRE/COLOR/HARMONICS, which is where these engines live. The played performance also can't be captured into the sequencer grid.

6. **Mono × 4 bars** *(both by design)* — worth stating only that the chord-shaped escape hatches already in the data soften it: SWx3/SQx3/TRx3/SIx3, WTx4, Plaits CHRD and STMC all produce chords from one voice, and the explain copy actively points users at them ("chords without polyphony," `plaits-models.ts:199`). Smart scoping.

## 4. Educational content assessment

**Overall: high quality, accurate, and genuinely pedagogical. A beginner would learn real synthesis vocabulary from it; an expert would trust ~95% of it.**

**Structure is doing real teaching work.** Every model gets three tiers: a one-line *what it is*, a "Listen for" line that tells your ear what to track as the knobs move, and a "Good for" line of musical application (`braids-models.ts:41-47`). That listen→apply split is textbook instructional design, and the live "Show me" sweep closes the loop with the ears.

**Accuracy spot-checks (against the vendored firmware and FM/synthesis theory):**

- Model ordering and indices match the firmware enum exactly (`braids-models.ts` vs `settings.h:37-95`) — the data file is structurally trustworthy as a reference.
- FM (index 25): *"Harmonic, musical tones when COLOR sits on a whole-number ratio (1:1, 2:1) and clangorous, bell-like inharmonics in between"* (`braids-models.ts:256`) — correct FM theory, taught in one sentence, with the actionable tip "detune COLOR a hair for bells."
- VOWL (index 22): *"COLOR shifting the formants up (child) or down (giant) as if resizing the singer's vocal tract"* (`braids-models.ts:232`) — accurate, and the vocal-tract analogy is exactly how formant shift is taught.
- RING (index 13): *"multiplying sines produces sum-and-difference partials"* (`braids-models.ts:160`) — correct ring-mod theory. **One found inaccuracy:** the description says "Two sine waves multiplied" while the knob text references a *second and third* sine; the firmware shape is `MACRO_OSC_SHAPE_TRIPLE_RING_MOD` (`settings.h:51`) — three oscillators. The knobs are right; the description undersells. Minor, but it's the kind of thing an expert spots.
- The Laxsynth copy is refreshingly honest about dead knobs: *"Little effect — noise bypasses the wavetable. Reach for SIZE (grain) and the filter"* (`laxsynth-models.ts:102-103`), and TRI's Mirror card admits *"Does almost nothing here… Use it on PULSE for real PWM"* (`laxsynth-models.ts:42`). Telling users what *doesn't* work builds exactly the trust a reference tool needs.

**Jargon gating: mostly good.** Terms like "paraphonic," "Q-factor," and "Dirac (pulse) comb" appear, but nearly always with an in-sentence gloss ("Dirac (pulse) comb," "it self-decays and is paraphonic, so fast lines stack their own ringing strings" — `braids-models.ts:84,281`). References (CS-80/Vangelis, Buchla/West Coast, PPG/Microwave, Solina, CZ-101, 808) assume some music-culture literacy but function as flavor, not prerequisites — a beginner loses nothing by skipping them, an enthusiast gets orientation from them.

**One systemic caveat:** the 2026-05-31 deep review flagged that the engine isn't yet bit-accurate (lo-fi chain order, internal AD envelope coverage, DRIFT). Where the explain copy describes firmware behavior the WASM doesn't fully reproduce, the "manual that knows what model you're on" can drift from what's heard. The envelope work has since landed for drums (`src/data/braids-envelopes.ts`), narrowing this, but it's the gap to watch for the reference-tool claim.

## 5. Under-leveraged assets

1. **The model catalogues are a Braids/Plaits encyclopedia trapped inside a synth.** ~870 lines of accurate, manual-faithful, conversationally-written documentation across `braids-models.ts`, `plaits-models.ts`, `laxsynth-models.ts` — arguably better organized than the official manuals (which don't have "Good for" lines). It's only readable one model at a time, in a side panel. A single browsable/printable "all models" reference view would make Parallax a bookmark for every Braids/Plaits owner *even when they don't want to play it* — and every visit funnels back into the instrument.

2. **Knob percentages are already a hardware recipe format.** The Explain panel and postcard both render exact macro percentages (`ExplainPanel.svelte:107`, `postcard.ts:123`). One sentence of framing ("set your module to these values") turns every shared patch into a *hardware* patch sheet — directly serving the strongest persona at zero engineering cost.

3. **Web MIDI is one CC-mapping away from being a performance instrument.** The note path is excellent; the message handler already parses CCs and deliberately drops them (`midi-input.ts:123-124`). Mapping CC1/CC74 (or a tiny learn mode) onto the current model's macro knobs converts "preview tool with a keyboard attached" into "instrument."

4. **The share-wire format is a preset interchange format that nobody can interchange.** URL hash, IndexedDB presets, and (potentially) files all use the same `encodeState` string (`persistence.ts:45`, `share-url.ts:39`). Export/import of preset packs — a "32 classic Braids patches" starter file, community packs — falls out of the existing format nearly for free.

5. **The analysis stack (`fft.ts`, `sample-analysis.ts`) is a general instrument-tuner/analyzer** — pitch, centroid, envelope, all pure and tested — currently reachable only through the Match panel. Even surfacing the detected pitch of *live mic input* someday would add a second standalone use.

## 6. Top 5 highest-utility-per-effort improvements

*(Respecting the deferred list: polyphony, audio recording/export, and insert FX are not demanded below — but see #5.)*

1. **Put the share URL on/with the postcard** (QR in the corner, or auto-copy the link whenever the PNG is downloaded/copied). Effort: hours. Turns the app's most shareable artifact from a screenshot into a working invitation. Files: `src/ui/postcard.ts`, `src/ui/PostcardModal.svelte`, reuse `buildShareUrl()`.

2. **MIDI CC → macro-knob mapping** (fixed CC numbers or simple learn). Effort: small — the handler and store-binding plumbing all exist (`midi-input.ts:115-126`, `state/bindings.ts`). Unlocks hardware performance of the parameters that define these engines.

3. **Preset library export/import as a `.json` file.** Effort: small (`persistence.ts` already stores the wire strings). Protects user work from browser-data loss and enables community preset packs — the cheapest possible network effect for a static-hosted app.

4. **Patch metadata in the exported .mid** (track name = "Parallax — Plaits MODL", text meta event with knob values and the share URL). Effort: trivial in `convert.ts:30-48` via `@tonejs/midi` track name. The DAW handoff stops losing the sound's identity.

5. **A browsable "Model Index" page from the existing data files** (all models, families, Listen for/Good for, linkable per model). Effort: a day; zero new content needed. This is the move that makes Parallax *useful to people who aren't even running it* — the reference tool the Eurorack community would actually link to. — And explicitly: **audio recording/export remains the single deferred item that gates the most personas** (sound designers, couch performers, social sharing). It is correctly deferred for v1 scope, but when v1 ships, it should be the first deferral revisited; a `MediaRecorder` tap on the existing master output is the small end of that wedge.
